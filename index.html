Puerto Rico · A first for the island

Healing burns close to home — before they cost a family everything.

An island of 3.2 million has no burn center of its own. The most serious cases — many of them children — are flown more than a thousand miles away. We're building the first comprehensive pediatric burn center in Puerto Rico's history.

The need

When a child is severely burned, two emergencies begin at once.

The first is medical. The second is financial. Today, the most serious cases leave the island for the mainland — and the bill that follows can ruin a family. One Puerto Rican patient was charged $1.7 million. A severe burn can take a child's skin; the debt that follows can take a family's future.

Our mission

We protect the children of Puerto Rico from the lasting harm of severe burns — healing their injuries close to home, and shielding their families from the medical debt that turns tragedy into poverty.

A model built to work here

One surgeon, multiplied across the island.

Serving the Entire Caribbean

Burn surgery is among the scarcest specialties in American medicine. So we don't wait for an impossible number of specialists — we extend the reach of a single expert.

  • A central hub provides surgery, critical care, and complex treatment — anchored by an expert burn surgeon.
  • A network of nurse-practitioner clinics delivers everyday wound care in communities across the island, close to home.
  • Telemedicine connects every clinic to the surgeon in real time, with clear paths to escalate when a child needs more.
  • Abundant, expertly trained NPs cost a fraction of a surgeon — making island-wide care both possible and sustainable.

Burns in the news

Why this work can't wait.

The cost of having no burn center of our own isn't abstract. It shows up in real families' lives.

CBS News2021

A Puerto Rican burn survivor's $1.7 million bill

A young man from Puerto Rico, burned over 70% of his body in a gas explosion, was flown to a Texas military hospital for seven months and 19 surgeries — then billed $1.7 million. The debt was waived only after members of Congress intervened.

Read the story at CBS News →

It should not take an act of Congress to help families impacted by burn trauma.

That's the gap we exist to close — expert burn care on the island, and a safety net so no family faces that bill alone.
